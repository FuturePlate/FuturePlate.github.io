The need

Most adult illness is written, in pencil, in childhood.

Non-communicable diseases like diabetes, heart disease and obesity are rising worldwide — and poor dietary habits formed early in life are a key driver. Children today grow up in food environments that crowd out the nutrients they need most.

Habits that follow us

Low fruit and vegetable intake in adolescence rarely stays in childhood — it persists into adulthood, quietly raising long-term health risks.

A rural gap

In rural Sri Lanka, structured nutrition education is limited. Awareness campaigns arrive in fits and starts — too inconsistent to change behaviour for good.

Knowing isn't doing

Even where knowledge exists, social and environmental pressures keep it from crossing into daily practice. Theory alone isn't enough.

Schools change everything

A school is a structured space where knowledge, attitudes and behaviour can be shaped together — repeatedly, with peers, woven into the day.

FuturePlate grew out of evidence, not a hunch. It was inspired by undergraduate research — “Assessment of adolescent behaviour on fruit and vegetable consumption in a rural area of Anuradhapura, Sri Lanka” — which revealed significant gaps in how adolescents eat.

The program

A practical, student-centred initiative

FuturePlate runs in selected government schools across the Anuradhapura District, focused on Grade 8 and 9 students and their teachers — because early adolescence is when lifelong habits are still soft enough to shape. It is backed by academic institutions, health professionals and educational authorities.

What we set out to achieve

Lifelong habits

Instil healthy eating that endures, with positive attitudes toward nutritious food.

More fruit & veg

Encourage greater daily consumption of fruits and vegetables among students.

Lower NCD risk

Reduce long-term risk of non-communicable diseases through early education.

Teachers & parents

Build teacher capacity and engage parents so habits are reinforced at home.

How it works

Three ways we reach a generation

FuturePlate steps away from the lecture and toward participation — interactive, peer-led and connected to the home.

01

Learning that doesn't feel like a lesson

Workshops run on quizzes, games, storytelling and vivid visual aids. Sessions cover balanced diets, the importance of fruits and vegetables, hydration and healthy-lifestyle habits — so students retain what they learn and carry it home.

02

School Nutrition Ambassador Program

Selected students are trained as peer leaders. Ambassadors run nutrition-themed campaigns, healthy cooking sessions and poster competitions, give short talks at school assemblies, and fill knowledge boards with their own creative content. A message lands differently when it comes from a classmate.

03

Community & parental engagement

Because the home environment carries real weight, FuturePlate offers parental awareness sessions on request — helping families understand balanced diets and support healthy eating under their own roof.

From the field

What we've seen so far

In its first phase, FuturePlate has run successfully in five schools across the Anuradhapura District — early days that have taught us a great deal.

Enthusiastic

Students threw themselves into workshops, lighting up during quizzes, games and competitions.

Welcomed

Teachers appreciated the program and named a long-felt gap: no structured nutrition education in the curriculum.

Needed

Many students had limited prior knowledge of basic nutrition — confirming exactly why this work matters.

Moments from our schools

Measuring what matters

A structured framework tracks impact: baseline and endline assessments capture students' knowledge, attitudes and practices. Qualitative feedback from students and teachers, plus observation checklists and ambassador activity logs, round out the picture — and every finding feeds back into the program.

Honest challenges

Crowded timetables, resource constraints and field logistics all create friction, and sustaining parental involvement remains the hardest part — because change at home decides whether anything lasts. The program has proven adaptable, refined lesson by lesson.

The road ahead

A foundation, not a one-off

FuturePlate was never meant to flare and fade. The path forward leads to more schools across the Anuradhapura District — and, in time, to other regions of Sri Lanka.
